---

### STAGE 0: THE GREAT FREEZE (The Concept)

### 1. The Relation: "Frozen Weights"

This is the single most important concept to understand about Usage.

- **In the Previous Step (Training):** The "Knobs" (Parameters) were loose. We were constantly twisting them with the Optimizer to fix errors. The model was "liquid."
- **In This Step (Usage):** We apply **Superglue** to every single knob.
    - **The Parameters are FROZEN.**
    - The model **cannot** learn anything new during Usage. If you tell it "My name is John," and then close the chat window, it forgets you immediately. It does not update its parameters to "remember" John.

### 2. The Shared Components

You use the exact same artifacts you built in the Factory:

1. **The Vocabulary:** You must use the *exact* same Dictionary. If ID 50 was "Apple" in training, you can't change it now.
2. **The Parameters:** You load the matrices we spent millions of dollars optimizing.

---

### STAGE 1: THE MECHANISM (The "Autoregressive" Loop)

How does the Transformer work when a user actually talks to it?

**The Goal:** The user types "The cat." The model must write a story.

**The Loop (Step-by-Step):**

### Turn 1

1. **User Input:** "The cat"
2. **Tokenization:** Converts to `[The, cat]`.
3. **Transformer Forward Pass:**
    - The vectors flow through the **Frozen Layers**.
    - Attention checks the relation between "The" and "cat".
    - FFN thinks about small animals.
4. **Output Logits:** It predicts the next word scores.
    - "sits": 80%
    - "flies": 1%
5. **Selection:** It picks **"sits"**.

### Turn 2 (The Relation to "Memory")

Now, here is the trick. The Transformer has no "memory" of what it just said *unless* we feed it back in.

1. **New Input:** We take the original input ("The cat") AND paste the new word ("sits").
    - **New Input:** "The cat **sits**"
2. **Transformer Forward Pass:**
    - Now Attention looks at "cat" AND "sits".
3. **Output Logits:**
    - "on": 90%
4. **Selection:** Picks **"on"**.

### Turn 3

1. **New Input:** "The cat sits **on**"
2. **Prediction:** "the"

**Crucial Point:** The model is effectively "re-reading" the entire conversation from the beginning every single time it generates a new word.

### STAGE 2: THE "USELESS" PARTS

When we move from Creation to Usage, we **throw away** half the machinery.

| Component | Status in Usage | Why? |

## Encoder and Decoder

Most modern models are just Encoders (BERT) or just Decoders (GPT). But when they work **Together** (like in Google Translate or T5), it is a beautiful relay race.

Here is the **Encoder-Decoder Architecture**.

---

### STAGE 0: THE ANALOGY (The Courtroom Reporter)

Imagine a courtroom.

1. **The Witness (Source Text):** speaks in German.
2. **The Court Reporter (The Encoder):** Listens to the German, understands it perfectly, and writes down a condensed summary (The Context Vector). They do *not* speak. They just listen and document.
3. **The Translator (The Decoder):** Reads the Reporter's summary and speaks out loud in English.

---

### STAGE 1: THE MECHANISM (Step-by-Step Trace)

**The Goal:** Translate **"I am good"** (English) -> **"Je vais bien"** (French).

### PHASE 1: THE ENCODER (The Listener)

The Encoder runs **Once** for the whole sentence.

1. **Input:** `["I", "am", "good"]`.
2. **Process:** It uses Self-Attention.
    - "Good" looks at "I". "Am" looks at "Good".
    - It creates a deep understanding of the grammar and meaning.
3. **Output:** A list of Vectors called the **"Encoder Hidden States"** (or Memory Bank).
    - *Analogy:* This is the locked reference manual. "Here is exactly what the English sentence means."

### PHASE 2: THE DECODER (The Writer)

The Decoder runs **Autoregressively** (Looping), but with a twist. It has **Two Eyes**.

- **Eye 1 (Self-Attention):** Looks at what it has already written (French).
- **Eye 2 (Cross-Attention):** Looks at the Encoder's reference manual (English).

**Turn 1:**

1. **Input:** `[START]` token.
2. **Self-Attention:** "I am at the start."
3. **CROSS-ATTENTION (The Bridge):**
    - The Decoder looks at the **Encoder Memory**.
    - *Query:* "How do I start a sentence about 'I'?"
    - *Match:* It finds the vector for "I".
4. **Generation:** Produces word **"Je"**.

**Turn 2:**

1. **Input:** `[START, Je]` ("Je" means I).
2. **Self-Attention:** "I have written 'Je'."
3. **CROSS-ATTENTION (The Bridge):**
    - The Decoder looks at the **Encoder Memory** again.
    - *Query:* "I have the subject 'Je'. What is the verb in the English sentence?"
    - *Match:* It finds the vector for "am".
4. **Generation:** Produces word **"vais"** (go/am).

**Turn 3:**

1. **Input:** `[..., Je, vais]`.
2. **CROSS-ATTENTION:**
    - *Query:* "Subject... Verb... What is the state?"
    - *Match:* Finds vector for "good".
3. **Generation:** Produces **"bien"**.

---

### STAGE 2: THE "CROSS-ATTENTION" BRIDGE

This is the technical component that makes them work together.
You know **Query, Key, and Value**?

In a standard GPT (Decoder only):

- Query, Key, and Value all come from the **User's Prompt** (The Decoder).

In an **Encoder-Decoder**:

1. **The Queries (Q)** come from the **Decoder** ("What do I need to write next?").
2. **The Keys (K) and Values (V)** come from the **Encoder** ("Here is the Source information").

**The Handshake:**
The Decoder is effectively searching a database created by the Encoder to find the facts it needs to write the translation.
