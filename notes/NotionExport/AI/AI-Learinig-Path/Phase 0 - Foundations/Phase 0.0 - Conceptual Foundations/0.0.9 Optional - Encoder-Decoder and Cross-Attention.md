## 🗺️ Stage 0 — Concept Map

0.0.3 named three transformer families: encoder, decoder, and **encoder-decoder**. This lesson is
the third one and the mechanism that powers it — **cross-attention**. It's mostly awareness (used by
translation/summarisation models like T5/BART), but *"what's cross-attention?"* and *"encoder vs
decoder?"* are common interview questions.

## 🔑 New Terms (plain English)

- **Encoder-decoder** — a model that **reads** an input fully, then **writes** a transformed output
  (translation, summarisation).
- **Encoder hidden states** — the encoder's "memory bank": its understanding of the whole input.
- **Self-attention** — words attending to **other words in the same sequence** (0.0.7).
- **Cross-attention** — the decoder attending to the **encoder's** memory while it writes.

## 🎈 Stage 1 — The Simple Idea (analogy: court reporter + translator)

Picture a courtroom. The **court reporter (encoder)** listens to the entire German testimony and
writes a precise condensed summary — they don't speak, they just *understand and record*. The
**translator (decoder)** then reads that summary and speaks the English, word by word. The
translator constantly **glances back at the reporter's notes** to stay faithful — that glancing back
is **cross-attention**.

**The "Aha!":** an encoder-decoder splits the job — one part *understands the input*, the other
*generates the output* while **looking back** at the understanding.

## ⚙️ Stage 2 — How It Actually Works

- **Encoder runs once** over the whole input → produces the **hidden states** (memory bank): a rich
  representation of what the input *means*.
- **Decoder runs autoregressively** (one word at a time, like 0.0.7) but with **two** attentions:
  1. **Self-attention** — looks at what it has written so far.
  2. **Cross-attention** — looks at the **encoder's memory**. The **Query** comes from the
     **decoder** ("what do I need next?"); the **Key** and **Value** come from the **encoder** ("here
     is the source meaning"). That handshake keeps the output tied to the input.

### 📊 The flow
```
  source ─►[ Encoder ]─► memory (hidden states)
                                │ (Key / Value)
  target word ◄─[ Decoder: self-attention + cross-attention(Query) ]◄─┘   ↺ one word at a time
```

## 🚀 Stage 3 — In Practice / Why It Matters (awareness)

Encoder-decoder models (T5, BART, MarianMT) shine at **translation and summarisation**. But for
**RAG and agents** you don't use them: you use a **decoder-only** LLM to generate **and** a separate
**encoder** embedding model to search — two specialised models, not one encoder-decoder. Knowing the
distinction keeps you from picking the wrong tool class in an architecture review (ties back to
0.0.6).

## 🧠 Common Misconceptions

- **"RAG uses an encoder-decoder model."** → No — RAG pairs a **separate** encoder (embeddings) with
  a **decoder** LLM (generation); they're different models working together (0.0.3).
- **"Cross-attention is just self-attention."** → No — in cross-attention the **Key/Value come from
  the encoder**, not from the decoder's own sequence.
- **"Decoder-only models can't 'see' the prompt."** → They can — via self-attention over the prompt;
  they just don't have a *separate* encoder.

## 📌 Quick Reference (key facts)

- **encoder-decoder** = read input fully → write output (translation/summarisation; T5, BART).
- **self-attention:** Q, K, V all from the **same** sequence.
- **cross-attention:** **Q from the decoder**, **K/V from the encoder**.
- for **RAG/agents** you use decoder-only LLM + a separate encoder embedding model — *not*
  encoder-decoder.

> 🎯 **Interview angle:** "Difference between self- and cross-attention?" → self-attention is within
> one sequence; cross-attention lets the decoder query the encoder's representation of the input.

## 🛑 STOP — Self-Check

In an encoder-decoder's **cross-attention**, where do the **Query** and the **Key/Value** come from,
and why does that arrangement make sense?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The **Query comes from the decoder** and the **Key/Value come from the encoder**. It makes sense
because the decoder is the one *asking* "what do I need from the source to write my next word?"
(the Query), while the encoder holds the *source meaning* to be looked up (the Keys to match
against, and the Values to pull in). So the decoder searches the encoder's memory bank as it writes.
</details>
